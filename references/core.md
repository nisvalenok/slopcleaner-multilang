# Core: Tells That Cross All Languages

These are structural, not lexical — they live in sentence architecture, not vocabulary — so they apply equally in English, German, and Russian. Work through these first, then open the language file.

## 1. False agency

An inanimate thing performs a human action. A complaint "becomes" a fix; a decision "emerges"; culture "shifts"; the data "tells" you something. Nothing did anything — a person acted. LLMs default to this because it sidesteps naming who.

- EN: "the complaint becomes a fix" → "the support team fixed it that week"
- DE: "die Entscheidung entsteht" → "das Team entscheidet"
- RU: «решение возникает» → «команда решает»

Fix: name the human. If none fits, put the reader in the seat with "you".

**Not this tell:** deliberate personification in literary or descriptive registers ("the wind tore at the shore", "der Sturm riss am Ufer"). The tell is *abstractions* wearing human verbs ("the analysis concludes", "die Vielfalt brach auf", «анализ показывает»), not vivid concrete imagery. When in doubt, ask whether a reader pictures a thing moving (fine) or an abstract noun acting (tell).

## 2. Vague declaratives

A sentence announces importance without naming the specific thing.

- EN: "The implications are significant." / "The reasons are structural."
- DE: "Das spielt eine wichtige Rolle." / "Es ist von großer Bedeutung."
- RU: «Это имеет большое значение.» / «Причины носят структурный характер.»

Fix: cut it, or state the actual implication. A sentence calling something important/deep/structural without showing the specific thing is filler.

## 3. Triads by reflex

LLMs reach for three parallel items — three adjectives, three clauses, three examples — as a default rhythm. The honest nuance (Wikipedia's own editors insist on it): the rule of three is legitimate, human, and old; marketers and speechwriters use it well. The tell is not the triad but the **reflex** — every list arriving in threes — and especially using a triad to make a thin point sound thorough.

Fix: where it's reflex or padding, drop to two items or one. Where a triad carries three real, distinct things, keep it. (German has a sharper sub-case — dash + three adjectives — see `de.md`.)

## 4. Manufactured profundity

Fragments deployed for weight. "[Noun]. That's it. That's the thing." Short fragments stacked for drama. A build-up resolving into one portentous word.

Fix: complete sentences. Let the content carry the weight instead of the punctuation.

## 5. No standpoint

The text arranges facts but holds no position, no experience, no claim of its own. This is the deepest tell and the least mechanical to fix — it separates writing that knows something from writing that lists things. Editors across all three languages converge on it: correct sentences, zero insight, no concrete detail, no surprising thought.

Fix: add a real standpoint, a lived detail, a specific example. If the writer or brief genuinely has none, that absence is the actual problem — name it rather than smoothing over it.

## 6. Filler intro and recap conclusion

The opening is a generic ramp ("In a world where…", "In der heutigen Zeit…", «В современном мире…»). The close restates what was already said with no new thought ("In summary…", "Abschließend lässt sich sagen…", «Подведём итог…"). No one writes a conclusion that only repeats; no one reads one.

Fix: open inside the matter. End on a real consequence, a next step, or a genuine last thought — never a restatement.

## 7. Glossy impersonality

Too smooth to be human: even sentence lengths, flawless grammar, no friction, no idiosyncrasy, no concrete particular — no Tuesday, no specific street, no brand name. The absence of any rough edge is itself the signal.

Fix: vary sentence length, let one run long and the next land short, allow a real writer's small irregularities, and anchor abstractions in one concrete detail.

---

## Rhythm across languages

Metronomic rhythm — every sentence the same length, every paragraph ending on a punchy line — reads as machine in all three languages. Mixing lengths is invariant. *How* you mix differs: English favors shorter sentences with sharp breaks; German and Russian tolerate long subordinate chains. The language-specific rhythm guidance lives in each language file.
